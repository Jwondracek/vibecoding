# 50-State (Plus DC and ABA) Survey of Legal Ethics Opinions on Lawyer Due Diligence for Cloud / Third-Party Technology Vendors

## TL;DR

- A clear national consensus exists: at least 32 U.S. states have issued lawyer-ethics opinions concluding that lawyers may use cloud, SaaS, and other third-party technology vendors so long as the lawyer undertakes reasonable due diligence on the vendor's confidentiality, security, backup, breach-notification, and data-retrieval practices; the ABA reinforces the same theme in Formal Opinions 477R (2017), 483 (2018), 498 (2021), and 512 (released July 29, 2024 per ABA press release).
- The specific three-part checklist articulated in Washington State Bar Advisory Opinion 2215 (2012) and quoted in Ohio Board of Professional Conduct Opinion 2017-5 (confidentiality agreement; backup; subpoena/breach notice and reliable data-retrieval) is most closely tracked by opinions from New York (842), New Jersey (701), Oregon (2011-188), North Carolina (2011 FEO 6), Pennsylvania (2011-200), Florida (12-3), Iowa (11-01), Maine (207), New Hampshire (2012-13/4), Illinois (16-06), and Nebraska (19-01); other states adopt the same "reasonable efforts" framing but with less granular factor lists.
- Eighteen states plus DC have not issued a dedicated cloud/SaaS lawyer-ethics opinion (Arkansas, Delaware, Georgia, Hawaii, Idaho, Indiana, Kansas, Maryland, Minnesota, Montana, New Mexico, Oklahoma, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Utah, West Virginia, Wyoming, and the District of Columbia); however, those jurisdictions are still bound by Model Rule 1.1 comment [8] technology-competence amendments and Model Rule 1.6(c)'s reasonable-efforts duty as adopted into their professional conduct rules.

## Key Findings

The doctrinal architecture is consistent across jurisdictions and is built on three pillars: Rule 1.1 with comment [8] on technology competence; Rule 1.6 confidentiality and its subsection (c) reasonable-efforts mandate; and Rule 5.3 supervision of nonlawyer assistants, including outside vendors. Every opinion that addresses cloud, SaaS, internet, or email vendors anchors its analysis in some combination of these three rules. The "reasonable efforts" formulation is universal; what varies is the granularity of the factor list each opinion provides.

The Washington Advisory Opinion 2215 (2012) checklist, as quoted by Ohio Opinion 2017-5, contains three operative elements: (1) the vendor must understand and agree to maintain and secure stored data consistent with the duty of confidentiality; (2) the vendor must back up client files and data regularly; and (3) the agreement must require notice of subpoenas, breaches, and unauthorized access, and must provide a reliable means of retrieving data if the agreement ends or the vendor goes out of business. The most faithful adopters of this granular formula are New York 842, New Jersey 701, Florida 12-3, Oregon 2011-188, North Carolina 2011 FEO 6, Pennsylvania 2011-200, Iowa 11-01, Illinois 16-06, and Nebraska 19-01. Other opinions adopt the general "reasonable care" framing without enumerating each element with this specificity.

The ABA opinions tie the theme into a single doctrinal throughline. ABA Formal Opinion 477R (2017) extends the reasonable-efforts duty from email to all internet-based communication and requires due diligence on communication-technology vendors. ABA Formal Opinion 483 (2018) imposes pre-breach monitoring obligations and post-breach client-notification duties anchored in Rules 1.1, 1.4, 1.6, 5.1, and 5.3. ABA Formal Opinion 498 (2021) confirms that virtual practice is permitted but requires vendor vetting, encryption where appropriate, regular data backup, and supervision policies. ABA Formal Opinion 512 (2024) extends the same Rule 5.3 vendor-vetting duty to generative AI providers, expressly carrying forward the cloud-computing and outsourcing diligence framework.

## Details: ABA Opinions (Doctrinal Tie-Together)

**ABA Formal Op. 477R (2017), "Securing Communication of Protected Client Information."** Revises Formal Op. 99-413. Requires fact-based analysis under Rule 1.6(c) and Rule 1.1 comment [8] to determine when extra security (encryption, secure portals) is needed when transmitting client data over the internet. Explicitly carries the outsourcing-due-diligence factors of ABA Formal Op. 08-451 (2008) into the analysis of vendor selection. Working PDF: https://docs.tbpr.org/pub/aba%20formal%20opinion%20477.authcheckdam.pdf.

**ABA Formal Op. 483 (2018), "Lawyers' Obligations After an Electronic Data Breach or Cyberattack."** Imposes both pre-breach monitoring obligations (under Rules 1.1, 1.6, 5.1, 5.3) and post-breach mitigation, investigation, and client-notification duties (under Rules 1.4 and 1.6). Standard is reasonableness, not strict liability. Working PDF: https://www.americanbar.org/content/dam/aba/administrative/professional_responsibility/ethics-opinions/aba-formal-op-483.pdf.

**ABA Formal Op. 498 (2021), "Virtual Practice."** Confirms virtual practice is permitted; requires regular backup, breach response plans, hardware/software terms-of-service review, video-conferencing diligence (confidentiality of recordings), document-exchange platform vetting, and listener-enabled device caution. Working PDF: https://www.lawnext.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/09/aba-formal-opinion-498.pdf.

**ABA Formal Op. 512 (released July 29, 2024 per ABA press release), "Generative Artificial Intelligence Tools."** Applies Rule 5.3 reasonable-efforts duty to generative AI providers and expressly carries forward the diligence framework from the ABA's prior cloud-computing and outsourcing opinions, covering reference checks, security policies, confidentiality agreements, conflicts screening, and whether the provider retains or claims proprietary rights to submitted content. Working PDF: https://www.lawnext.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/aba-formal-opinion-512.pdf.

**Older ABA building blocks.** ABA Formal Op. 95-398 (1995) (vendor access to lawyer's database) is the original ancestor of the duty: it required service providers to have reasonable procedures to protect confidentiality and a written assurance of confidentiality. ABA Formal Op. 08-451 (2008) on outsourcing established the multi-factor vendor-diligence framework that Opinions 477R, 483, 498, and 512 carry forward.

## Details: Synthesis

**(a) How many states have squarely adopted the vendor-vetting concept?** Thirty-two states have issued dedicated cloud/SaaS/third-party-vendor ethics opinions for lawyers that adopt the reasonable-efforts vendor-vetting concept (Alabama, Alaska, Arizona, California, Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Illinois, Iowa, Kentucky, Louisiana, Maine, Massachusetts, Michigan, Mississippi, Missouri, Nebraska, Nevada, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, North Dakota, Ohio, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Tennessee, Texas, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, and Wisconsin). All thirty-two are unanimous: lawyers may use cloud and SaaS provided they perform reasonable due diligence on the vendor's confidentiality, security, backup, and data-retrieval practices.

**(b) Which states most closely match the Washington-style three-part checklist?** Eleven jurisdictions adopt the granular three-part formulation explicitly or with near-identical wording: Washington (Op. 2215, the source); Ohio (Op. 2017-5, which expressly quotes Washington); New York (Op. 842); New Jersey (Op. 701); Oregon (Op. 2011-188); North Carolina (2011 FEO 6); Pennsylvania (Op. 2011-200, with a fifteen-point safeguard list that exceeds the three-part standard); Florida (Op. 12-3); Iowa (Op. 11-01); New Hampshire (Op. 2012-13/4); Illinois (Op. 16-06); and Nebraska (Op. 19-01).

**(c) Which states have no opinion?** Eighteen states plus the District of Columbia have no dedicated cloud/SaaS lawyer-ethics opinion: Arkansas, Delaware, Georgia, Hawaii, Idaho, Indiana, Kansas, Maryland, Minnesota, Montana, New Mexico, Oklahoma, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Utah, West Virginia, and Wyoming, plus D.C. Several of these jurisdictions have nevertheless adopted the technology-competence comment to Rule 1.1 and the reasonable-efforts language of Rule 1.6(c), making the same duty enforceable in practice; D.C. did so by formal rule amendment (Order No. M284-24, issued April 7, 2025; effective May 6, 2025).

**(d) How do the ABA opinions tie the theme together?** The ABA opinions provide the doctrinal spine. ABA Op. 95-398 (1995) and Op. 08-451 (2008) created the vendor-diligence framework; Op. 477R (2017) carried it forward to internet communication; Op. 483 (2018) imposed pre-breach monitoring and post-breach client notification; Op. 498 (2021) confirmed the framework's application to virtual practice; and Op. 512 (2024) extends it to generative AI tools. ABA Op. 512 explicitly cites the prior cloud-computing and outsourcing opinions, demonstrating that the ABA views the cloud-vendor and AI-vendor diligence duties as a single continuum.

**(e) The doctrinal throughline.** The duty rests on three intersecting rules. Model Rule 1.1, comment [8] (technology competence) was adopted by exactly 41 jurisdictions as of April 2025 (LawNext, citing D.C. Court of Appeals Order No. M284-24: "the District of Columbia is now the forty-first jurisdiction to explicitly add technology considerations to its professional ethics code"), and requires lawyers to keep abreast of the benefits and risks of relevant technology. Model Rule 1.6(c) (reasonable efforts to prevent inadvertent or unauthorized disclosure) operationalizes the technology-competence duty when client confidences are at stake. Model Rule 5.3 (responsibilities regarding nonlawyer assistance) extends supervisory duties to nonlawyers outside the firm, including cloud and SaaS vendors; Rule 5.3, comment [3] is the textual hook for the vendor-vetting duty in the Ohio and Washington opinions. The "reasonable efforts" standard is universal, not strict liability; the standard adjusts to the sensitivity of the data, the risk of disclosure, the cost of additional safeguards, the difficulty of implementation, and the impact on representation (Model Rule 1.6 comment [18]).

## Recommendations

1. **For lawyers in any U.S. jurisdiction:** Treat the Washington Op. 2215 / Ohio Op. 2017-5 three-part checklist as the de facto national minimum. Before adopting any cloud, SaaS, email, AI, or internet-based vendor, confirm in writing that (i) the vendor agrees to maintain confidentiality consistent with the lawyer's Rule 1.6 duty; (ii) the vendor regularly backs up client data; and (iii) the contract provides notice of subpoenas, unauthorized access, and breaches, plus a reliable means of retrieving data on termination or vendor failure. Document the diligence in a vendor file.

2. **For lawyers in states with no dedicated cloud opinion (Arkansas, Delaware, Georgia, Hawaii, Idaho, Indiana, Kansas, Maryland, Minnesota, Montana, New Mexico, Oklahoma, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Utah, West Virginia, Wyoming, and D.C.):** Apply the ABA Formal Opinion 477R, 483, 498, and 512 framework, plus your state's adoption of Rule 1.1 comment [8] and Rule 1.6(c). Disciplinary authorities in those jurisdictions will look to ABA and sister-state opinions as persuasive authority.

3. **Threshold for elevated diligence:** When client data is particularly sensitive (M&A, criminal defense, trade secrets, health/PII, attorney-client communications protected by privilege, or any matter where breach would materially prejudice the client), elevate the diligence: require encryption in transit and at rest; require SOC 2 Type II reports current within twelve months; require contractually committed data-residency jurisdictions; verify tenant isolation; and obtain informed client consent before storing or transmitting via the internet (the Massachusetts Op. 12-03 standard).

4. **Trigger for re-evaluation:** Re-vet every vendor at least annually and immediately upon any material change in vendor ownership, terms of service, training-data policy, or sub-processor list. The Washington Op. 2215 expressly requires this; ABA Op. 512 echoes it for AI providers.

5. **Trigger for client consent and disclosure:** Disclose vendor use to the client when (i) the data is highly sensitive; (ii) the vendor's terms of service permit secondary use of client data (e.g., AI training); (iii) the engagement involves regulated data (PHI, financial, classified); or (iv) the client has expressed any preference about technology. Boilerplate engagement-letter provisions are not sufficient under ABA Op. 512.

## Caveats

- The North Dakota Op. 99-03 citation is acknowledged in secondary citation lists (e.g., NYSBA Op. 1020 footnote 2) but a primary-source URL was not independently confirmed on the State Bar Association of North Dakota website; treat the citation as verified by secondary authority only.
- Missouri publishes only summaries of its informal opinions; the full text of Mo. Bar Informal Op. 2018-09 is not publicly available, only summary references.
- Arizona Op. 09-04 was issued under prior ethics-committee authority; the Arizona Supreme Court's Attorney Ethics Advisory Committee, created in 2018, has not formally re-adopted past opinions, though the substantive guidance remains a useful reference.
- Wisconsin Op. EF-15-01 is behind a member login wall; the public-facing URL requires authentication.
- The technology-competence count of exactly 41 jurisdictions reflects the position as of April 2025; Puerto Rico Supreme Court Order ER-2025-02 (June 17, 2025) adopted new Rule 1.19 ("Technological Competence and Diligence") effective January 1, 2026, raising the prospective count to 42 (per Redgrave LLP 2025 Update).
- Several jurisdictions have published opinions on adjacent topics (email confidentiality, virtual practice, data breach, generative AI) that effectively impose the same vendor-vetting duty even without a dedicated cloud opinion; this report treats only opinions whose core subject is cloud, SaaS, or third-party tech vendor diligence as "Yes."
- Citations follow Bluebook ethics-opinion form: issuing body, opinion number, year. Where states use distinctive titles (e.g., "Advisory Opinion," "Informal Opinion," "Formal Ethics Opinion"), the report uses the title employed by the issuing body.
